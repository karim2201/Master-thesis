\chapter{General Conclusion and Future Work}

\section{General Conclusion}
This thesis studied the Digital Twin paradigm in the context of eHealth. It showed that Digital Twin technology can support the transition from fragmented digital health applications toward dynamic, personalized, and service-oriented healthcare systems. A Digital Twin is not only a digital copy or a visualization tool; it is a connected virtual representation that uses data, models, and services to support monitoring, simulation, prediction, and decision-making.

The literature review showed that Digital Twins for Health are receiving growing attention, but the field remains emerging. Existing works provide valuable definitions, classifications, architectures, and use cases. However, many systems are still conceptual, prototype-based, or limited to specific scenarios. The implementation of reliable eHealth Digital Twins requires interoperability, security, privacy, clinical validation, explainability, and ethical governance.

To address these needs, this thesis proposed a general multi-layer architecture for Digital Twin-based eHealth systems. The architecture includes physical, acquisition, interoperability, data management, virtual twin, intelligence, simulation, and service layers, with security and privacy as cross-cutting requirements.

\section{Limitations}
This thesis is primarily an academic state-of-the-art and conceptual architecture study. It does not provide a full clinical deployment or a validated medical product. The proposed architecture must be adapted and evaluated according to a specific healthcare use case, available data sources, regulatory constraints, and user requirements.

\section{Future Work}
Future work may focus on implementing and evaluating the proposed architecture in real or semi-real eHealth scenarios. Important directions include:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item integration with standardized EHR systems using HL7/FHIR;
    \item development of secure data acquisition pipelines from connected medical devices;
    \item design of explainable AI and simulation modules;
    \item validation using realistic healthcare datasets;
    \item evaluation of usability with healthcare professionals and patients;
    \item strengthening privacy, consent management, and ethical governance;
    \item extension toward multi-twin ecosystems connecting patients, devices, workflows, and healthcare services.
\end{itemize}

\section{Final Remarks}
Digital Twins for eHealth represent a promising research direction at the intersection of healthcare, computer science, data engineering, artificial intelligence, and systems modeling. Their success will depend not only on technical performance, but also on trust, interoperability, clinical relevance, and responsible design.
