\chapter{Comparative Study of Digital Twin Approaches in eHealth}

\section{Overview of Selected Studies}
The selected studies are organized into eight categories: Digital Twin foundations, Digital Twins for Health, healthcare architecture proposals, AI-enabled and hybrid Digital Twin approaches, patient-specific systems, interoperability sources, ethics and governance sources, and cardiovascular Digital Twin studies. This classification supports comparison rather than isolated paper summaries.

General Digital Twin papers clarify definitions, maturity levels, physical--virtual coupling, enabling technologies, and architecture patterns~\cite{fuller2020digital,kritzinger2018digital,tao2019industry,jones2020characterising,rasheed2020digital}. Healthcare-oriented reviews and perspectives explain adaptation to patient-specific modeling, precision medicine, clinical workflows, and public health~\cite{katsoulakis2024digital,bjornsson2019digital,venkatesh2022health,kamelboulos2021digital,dedomenico2025challenges}. Architecture-oriented studies support comparison between healthcare-unit, process-oriented, and patient-oriented designs~\cite{debenedictis2023digital,noeikham2024architecture,sharma2025patient}.

AI-oriented sources are included to distinguish general AI methodology, AI as an enabling component inside a Digital Twin, practical AI-powered healthcare Digital Twin applications, and hybrid AI--simulation models~\cite{janiesch2021MachineDeepLearning,chaparroCardenas2025DTAIHealthcare,elgammal2025AIPoweredHealthcareDT,sharma2025patient}. Cardiovascular studies are included as a representative clinical deep-dive because they combine biosignals, imaging, hemodynamics, electrophysiology, physiological simulation, and AI in patient-specific models~\cite{coorey2022health,corralacero2020digital,sel2024building,cluitmans2024electrophysiology,bhagirath2024bits,geddes2025right,gu2025heartfailure}. Ethical, interoperability, and validation sources are also included because trustworthy Digital Twins require responsible data use, explainability, standards-based integration, clinical validation, and human supervision~\cite{bruynseels2018digital,lehne2019interoperability,mandel2016smart,vayena2018machine,amann2020explainability,viceconti2020insilico}.

The comparison therefore follows a dual state-of-the-art logic within the same chapter. The first level examines eHealth Digital Twins as a broad class of systems, including patient twins, process twins, public-health twins, AI-enabled services, and governance requirements. The second level uses cardiovascular Digital Twins as a deeper clinical case because this domain exposes the practical difficulty of combining multimodal signals, imaging, anatomical reconstruction, pressure and flow measurements, mechanistic simulation, AI-based personalization, validation evidence, and clinical workflow constraints.

\section{Analytical Comparison Dimensions}
The comparison uses the following dimensions: represented entity or process, data modalities, modeling approach, synchronization direction, maturity level, services, evidence or validation strategy, interoperability, explainability, privacy, governance, and limitations. These dimensions distinguish conceptual frameworks, static models, digital shadows, partial Digital Twins, process Digital Twins, patient-specific calibrated models, and intelligent-DT visions.

This distinction is necessary because Digital Twin terminology remains inconsistent. Maturity labels are therefore used cautiously: a system is not treated as a complete real-time clinical Digital Twin unless it demonstrates continuous data exchange, synchronization with an active physical counterpart, virtual-state update, and clinically meaningful service integration.

Before comparing the selected studies, it is necessary to clarify how AI contributes to acquisition, modeling, prediction, personalization, simulation, and service functions, and why AI capability should not be confused with Digital Twin maturity.

\section{Artificial Intelligence in Digital Twin Systems for eHealth}
AI is not synonymous with a Digital Twin. A Digital Twin is a connected physical--virtual system in which a real entity, process, or patient state is represented, updated, and used to deliver services. AI is an enabling intelligence component that may operate inside this system, but a prediction model alone is not a Digital Twin. Conversely, a Digital Twin can exist without advanced AI, for example as a synchronized mechanistic model, rule-based representation, or process model.

AI becomes valuable when the twin must learn patterns from heterogeneous data, estimate hidden states, process images or biosignals, forecast trajectories, personalize parameters, or optimize decisions. However, the maturity of a Digital Twin depends on physical--virtual connection, synchronization, state updating, service integration, validation, and governance, not merely on the presence of AI. This distinction extends the maturity discussion introduced in Chapters 2 and 4: an AI classifier may be clinically useful, but it remains only one component unless it is embedded in a maintained twin that connects measured reality, virtual state, and user-facing services.

\subsection{Conceptual Relationship Between AI, ML, and DL}
Janiesch et al. distinguish AI as the broad field concerned with intelligent reasoning, learning, perception, planning, knowledge representation, and decision support, while ML refers to methods that learn analytical relationships from data rather than relying only on manually programmed rules~\cite{janiesch2021MachineDeepLearning}. Artificial neural networks are one family of ML models, and DL is the subset of ML based on deep artificial neural networks with multiple representation layers.

This distinction is important for eHealth Digital Twins because different data types require different modeling strategies. Structured clinical variables may be handled by regression, trees, support vector machines, probabilistic models, or clustering methods. Time-series biosignals may require temporal models. Medical images often benefit from convolutional or transformer-based models. Textual clinical notes, wearable streams, and longitudinal patient histories introduce additional requirements for representation learning, sequence modeling, and multimodal fusion.

DL can learn representations from high-dimensional inputs, but it is not automatically superior. Shallow ML may remain preferable for smaller, lower-dimensional, or tabular datasets, especially when interpretability, calibration, and auditability are central requirements. The most complex model is not necessarily the most appropriate model; model choice should depend on data type, sample size, clinical objective, latency constraints, explainability needs, and validation evidence~\cite{janiesch2021MachineDeepLearning,rudin2019blackbox}.

\subsection{Functional Roles of AI Within a Digital Twin}
AI can support several functions across the Digital Twin lifecycle. During data preprocessing and quality control, it may assist artifact detection, signal denoising, missing-value estimation, anomaly detection, image segmentation, feature extraction, data harmonization, and temporal alignment. These functions are useful for heterogeneous clinical data, but they must not conceal poor data quality or silently fabricate medically significant observations.

AI can also support state estimation by inferring variables that are difficult to observe directly, such as latent physiological state, disease severity, deterioration probability, patient phenotype, or hidden risk factors. These outputs must be distinguished from directly measured clinical values. In a trustworthy twin, measured values, model-estimated states, predicted values, and simulated values should remain traceable.

For pattern recognition and diagnostic assistance, AI may analyze biosignals, medical images, EHR variables, laboratory results, symptoms, and longitudinal patterns. This supports diagnostic assistance rather than autonomous medical diagnosis. For prediction and prognosis, AI may estimate disease progression, future physiological measurements, readmission risk, treatment response, adverse events, or patient trajectories. Such predictions require validation, calibration, uncertainty estimation, and external testing before clinical reliance.

AI can contribute to personalization and model calibration by adapting a general model to an individual using current measurements, historical records, demographic information, treatment history, and behavioral or contextual data. This is a key difference between a static cohort-level model and an evolving patient-specific twin. AI may also serve as a surrogate for expensive mechanistic simulation, reducing execution time for what-if scenarios. Surrogate models can improve computational feasibility, but they may lose physiological fidelity outside their training domain.

In optimization and decision support, AI may compare possible actions, rank scenarios, prioritize alerts, or support treatment and resource planning. Clinical decisions must remain human-supervised. More adaptive intelligent twins may update parameters as new information arrives, but online learning introduces risks of concept drift, model degradation, uncontrolled behavior, and difficult auditability. Adaptive models therefore require monitoring, revalidation, logging, and governance.

\subsection{AI Techniques Used in Healthcare Digital Twins}
Conventional ML methods such as regression, decision trees, random forests, support vector machines, clustering, k-nearest neighbors, and probabilistic models remain relevant for structured clinical data, patient stratification, risk assessment, and interpretable baselines~\cite{janiesch2021MachineDeepLearning}. They can be valuable when datasets are modest, variables are tabular, or clinicians need transparent feature relationships.

DL methods are frequently discussed for high-dimensional healthcare data. Convolutional neural networks can support imaging and signal morphology analysis; recurrent networks and LSTM models can process temporal signals; transformers can represent long-range temporal or multimodal dependencies; and autoencoders can support representation learning, denoising, and anomaly detection~\cite{janiesch2021MachineDeepLearning,chaparroCardenas2025DTAIHealthcare}. These models can improve pattern extraction but may require large datasets, careful validation, and stronger explainability controls.

Reinforcement learning and optimization methods may support sequential decision problems, adaptive resource allocation, and scenario ranking. In clinical care, however, autonomous reinforcement-learning treatment policies create substantial safety, ethical, and validation concerns. They should be treated as decision-support research unless prospective evidence, governance, and clinician supervision are established.

Generative AI has been explored for synthetic data generation, missing-data support, virtual cohorts, counterfactual scenarios, data augmentation, and possible patient trajectories~\cite{chaparroCardenas2025DTAIHealthcare,elgammal2025AIPoweredHealthcareDT}. Synthetic data may help prototype systems or explore rare scenarios, but they are not equivalent to real clinical evidence. Risks include hallucinated patterns, unrealistic samples, replicated bias, privacy leakage, and weak physiological constraints.

Recent healthcare Digital Twin research also discusses LLM-related approaches for processing longitudinal EHR information, clinical trajectory forecasting, clinician-facing interfaces, summarization, counterfactual scenario generation, and synthetic patient representation~\cite{elgammal2025AIPoweredHealthcareDT}. These systems remain emerging; many are experimental, retrospective, or dependent on synthetic data, and they should not be treated as established clinical Digital Twins.

Graph-based and network models can represent relationships among diagnoses, treatments, biomarkers, organs, physiological systems, patient similarity, and disease pathways. They may support relational reasoning and patient matching, but their graph structures and assumptions require validation. Hybrid AI and mechanistic modeling combine physiological simulation, data-driven learning, rules or symbolic knowledge, probabilistic inference, and optimization. Such hybrid approaches may improve physiological plausibility and interpretability while retaining predictive flexibility, as illustrated by patient-specific frameworks and cardiovascular studies~\cite{sharma2025patient,geddes2025right,gu2025heartfailure}.

\subsection{Multimodal and Hybrid Intelligence}
Healthcare Digital Twins frequently need to combine EHR records, biosignals, imaging, laboratory data, genomic or molecular data, wearable streams, medication history, patient-reported outcomes, behavioral information, and environmental context. Chaparro-Cárdenas et al. emphasize the relevance of multimodal AI and the emerging combination of deep learning with symbolic or rule-based reasoning for personalized and predictive healthcare~\cite{chaparroCardenas2025DTAIHealthcare}.

Multimodal fusion can occur through early fusion, feature-level fusion, model-level fusion, decision-level fusion, or attention-based mechanisms. The central challenge is not only technical integration but also clinical meaning. Modalities differ in sampling rate, missingness, uncertainty, measurement quality, and temporal resolution. A robust twin should preserve source provenance, timestamps, confidence, modality-specific uncertainty, and distinctions between direct measurements and inferred states.

\subsection{Trustworthiness, Validation, and Deployment Challenges}
AI-enabled Digital Twins inherit the usual challenges of healthcare AI while adding Digital Twin-specific requirements for synchronization, adaptation, and service integration. Data heterogeneity, limited and imbalanced datasets, missing longitudinal records, model bias, overfitting, dataset shift, concept drift, black-box behavior, insufficient explainability, weak uncertainty estimation, and calibration problems can all reduce trustworthiness~\cite{janiesch2021MachineDeepLearning,chaparroCardenas2025DTAIHealthcare,elgammal2025AIPoweredHealthcareDT}.

Chaparro-Cárdenas et al. highlight issues related to privacy, federated learning, edge computing, scalability, multimodal AI, and clinical validation in personalized and predictive healthcare~\cite{chaparroCardenas2025DTAIHealthcare}. Elgammal et al. emphasize black-box limitations, clinician acceptance, EHR integration, regulatory difficulty for continuously adaptive Digital Twins, lack of standardized validation, predictive uncertainty, and workflow integration~\cite{elgammal2025AIPoweredHealthcareDT}. Janiesch et al. support the methodological distinction between model construction, out-of-sample assessment, performance metrics, interpretability trade-offs, and computational requirements~\cite{janiesch2021MachineDeepLearning}.

Validation must therefore be layered. Predictive validation evaluates statistical performance on held-out and out-of-sample data. Physiological validation checks whether estimated states and simulated trajectories remain clinically and biologically plausible. External validation tests performance across institutions, devices, populations, and time periods. Prospective clinical validation studies safety and usefulness in real care settings. Workflow validation evaluates usability, latency, integration, alert burden, human--machine interaction, and clinician acceptance. Cross-validation alone is not sufficient to establish clinical validity.

\subsection{Critical Interpretation}
The reviewed AI literature leads to several critical conclusions. AI is an enabling component, not the defining criterion of a Digital Twin. ML and DL can transform raw multimodal data into estimates, predictions, and adaptive services, but mechanistic simulation remains important for physiological interpretation and what-if analysis. Hybrid AI--simulation systems are especially relevant for patient-specific healthcare because they can combine learning from data with domain constraints.

Generative AI, LLMs, and graph methods expand the possible service space of healthcare Digital Twins, but they remain emerging and require cautious validation. More complex AI does not automatically produce a more mature or clinically useful twin. Digital Twin maturity must still be evaluated through synchronization, personalization, validation, service integration, and physical--virtual coupling. Healthcare deployment also requires explainability, uncertainty communication, privacy, security, clinical validation, and human supervision. \section{Synthesis of Findings}
\subsection{Conceptual Findings}
The comparative analysis shows that Digital Twin terminology remains inconsistent. Foundational papers converge on the need for a physical entity, a virtual representation, and a meaningful connection between them, but healthcare literature often applies the term to systems with different levels of maturity~\cite{kritzinger2018digital,fuller2020digital,jones2020characterising}. Some studies describe static anatomical models, offline simulations, monitoring dashboards, or one-way data pipelines as Digital Twins. These systems may be valuable, but they should be distinguished from fully synchronized twins.

The distinction between a digital model, a digital shadow, a Digital Twin, and an intelligent Digital Twin is therefore central to the thesis. A true Digital Twin requires more than a simulation model; it requires a maintained relationship between the physical and virtual entities. Data and services are also important dimensions because the value of a twin depends on the ability to update, interpret, simulate, and expose useful outputs to users~\cite{tomczyk2022paradigm,tao2019industry}. In healthcare, maturity must also account for clinical safety, validation, interpretability, and workflow integration.

\subsection{Data Findings}
Healthcare Digital Twins require multimodal and longitudinal data. Patient-specific systems may combine EHR data, biosignals, imaging, laboratory values, medication history, wearable measurements, patient-reported information, and contextual variables~\cite{katsoulakis2024digital,venkatesh2022health,sharma2025patient}. The comparative literature shows that the main difficulty is not only collecting data, but aligning them temporally, validating their quality, and preserving their clinical meaning.

Measurements may be incomplete, contradictory, noisy, or collected at different sampling rates. Wearable and IoMT data can support continuous monitoring, but they introduce reliability, calibration, connectivity, and privacy concerns~\cite{dimitrov2016medical}. Interoperability remains a central limitation because clinical data are often fragmented across EHR systems, repositories, devices, and organizational boundaries~\cite{lehne2019interoperability,mandel2016smart}. Data provenance and quality must therefore be treated as architectural requirements, not as preprocessing details.

\subsection{Modeling Findings}
The reviewed studies show that mechanistic models and AI models provide different but complementary strengths. Mechanistic models improve physiological interpretability and can support what-if simulation, especially in domains such as cardiovascular mechanics, electrophysiology, and hemodynamics~\cite{corralacero2020digital,geddes2025right}. AI methods support data preprocessing, state estimation, pattern recognition, risk prediction, phenotyping, anomaly detection, personalization, and adaptation to complex multimodal data~\cite{gu2025heartfailure,sharma2025patient,chaparroCardenas2025DTAIHealthcare,elgammal2025AIPoweredHealthcareDT}.

The AI-oriented literature also shows that modeling choices should be matched to the data and clinical objective. Shallow ML may provide useful interpretable baselines for structured clinical variables, while DL is more appropriate for high-dimensional images, biosignals, or long temporal sequences when sufficient data and validation are available~\cite{janiesch2021MachineDeepLearning}. Emerging generative AI, LLM, and graph-based approaches can extend services toward synthetic cohorts, trajectory forecasting, relational reasoning, and clinician-facing interfaces, but these approaches remain sensitive to uncertainty, bias, privacy, and clinical validation limits~\cite{elgammal2025AIPoweredHealthcareDT}.

Hybrid approaches are promising because they combine data-driven prediction with physiological constraints derived from domain knowledge~\cite{rasheed2020digital,dedomenico2025challenges}. However, both approaches have limitations. Mechanistic models may be computationally expensive and difficult to calibrate for individual patients. AI models may be sensitive to bias, missing data, poor generalization, and model drift. The presence of a strong AI model should therefore not be confused with Digital Twin maturity; maturity still depends on physical--virtual coupling, state updating, synchronization, service integration, and validation. Uncertainty, interpretability, calibration, and monitoring of adaptive models must be represented and communicated when model outputs may influence clinical interpretation.

\subsection{Architectural Findings}
Architecture-oriented studies converge toward layered and modular healthcare Digital Twin designs. De Benedictis et al. propose a generalized healthcare architecture, while Noeikham et al. emphasize healthcare-unit integration, ICT systems, security, privacy, and service monitoring~\cite{debenedictis2023digital,noeikham2024architecture}. Broader Digital Twin architecture literature also separates the physical space, data acquisition, data management, virtual representation, analytics, service delivery, and connection mechanisms~\cite{tao2019industry,fuller2020digital}.

The synthesis of these studies indicates that a healthcare Digital Twin architecture should not be a monolithic system. Patient-specific twins and healthcare-unit twins have different modeling requirements, but both need data acquisition, interoperability, validated storage, virtual-state management, intelligence or simulation components, and user-facing services. Security, privacy, governance, explainability, traceability, and consent management must operate across all layers rather than being added as a final component.

\subsection{Derived Multi-Layer Architecture}
From the reviewed literature, seven recurring architectural layers can be synthesized for a general eHealth Digital Twin system:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Physical Layer}: represents the real healthcare environment, including patients, medical devices, sensors, clinicians, hospital units, and clinical processes.
    \item \textbf{Data Acquisition Layer}: collects data from connected sensors, electronic health records, laboratory results, imaging systems, wearable devices, and clinical inputs.
    \item \textbf{Interoperability and Preprocessing Layer}: cleans, validates, aligns, standardizes, and transforms heterogeneous data so that they can be used by the twin.
    \item \textbf{Data Management Layer}: stores structured and unstructured data, maintains longitudinal records, manages metadata, and supports secure access to patient-related information.
    \item \textbf{Virtual Twin Layer}: maintains the virtual representation of the patient, organ, device, process, or healthcare service being modeled.
    \item \textbf{Intelligence and Simulation Layer}: applies AI, analytics, mechanistic simulation, risk estimation, and what-if analysis to support interpretation and prediction.
    \item \textbf{Service Layer}: provides monitoring, visualization, alerts, reports, decision support, and controlled feedback to healthcare professionals, patients, or administrators.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cross-Cutting Requirements}
The literature shows that several requirements must affect every layer. Security, privacy, governance, interoperability, ethics, traceability, consent management, explainability, clinical validation, and human supervision are cross-cutting conditions for trustworthy deployment~\cite{bruynseels2018digital,vayena2018machine,char2018implementing,amann2020explainability}. For example, privacy begins during acquisition, continues through storage and modeling, and remains relevant when outputs are displayed or reused. Explainability depends not only on AI models but also on data provenance, model assumptions, uncertainty communication, and clinical interpretation.

\subsection{Clinical and Cardiovascular Findings}
Most healthcare Digital Twin systems remain conceptual, retrospective, simulated, or proof-of-concept. Prospective clinical validation is still uncommon, and bidirectional autonomous feedback is rare. This does not reduce the value of the research, but it requires cautious terminology. A system that predicts risk from retrospective data is not equivalent to a clinically deployed and continuously synchronized patient twin.

Cardiovascular Digital Twins demonstrate the value of combining biosignals, imaging, pressure or flow measurements, electrophysiological data, and physiological models. The cardiovascular literature includes field mapping, precision-cardiology visions, clinical-impact frameworks, edge prototypes, electrophysiology twins, whole-heart models, hemodynamic simulations, and heart-failure phenotyping~\cite{coorey2022health,corralacero2020digital,sel2024building,cluitmans2024electrophysiology,bhagirath2024bits,martinez2019cardio,geddes2025right,gu2025heartfailure}. These studies show how hybrid modeling can transform heterogeneous measurements into clinically interpretable features. At the same time, many cardiovascular works are patient-specific simulations or calibrated models rather than fully synchronized clinical Digital Twins.

Three cardiovascular modeling directions can be distinguished. Electrophysiological twins simulate activation, repolarization, arrhythmia induction, drug response, and ablation scenarios~\cite{arevalo2016arrhythmia,prakosa2018virtualheart,zhang2023genotype,hwang2024amiodarone,ohara2024mri}. Electromechanical and hemodynamic twins estimate pressures, flows, volumes, tissue mechanics, circulation, and device-response scenarios~\cite{gerach2021electromechanical,viola2023gpu,geddes2025right}. Hybrid and AI-enhanced cardiovascular twins use mechanistic parameters as interpretable features, create population-scale research cohorts, or train surrogate models to accelerate expensive simulations~\cite{gu2025heartfailure,qian2025populations,salvador2024latent}. The strongest emerging systems combine these directions, but every added modality increases calibration, identifiability, uncertainty, computational, and validation challenges.

The cardiovascular evidence also clarifies the difference between clinical relevance and Digital Twin maturity. Some electrophysiology studies validate simulated circuits or treatment scenarios against procedural or follow-up data, which is stronger than a purely conceptual model. However, most remain retrospective or offline, with small cohorts, disease-specific assumptions, or limited longitudinal updating. Population-scale cardiac twinning shows that personalization can be extended to large research cohorts, but episodic research measurements are not equivalent to persistent synchronization in routine care. AI surrogates improve computational feasibility, yet their domain of validity must be controlled because fast simulation is not the same as clinically validated prediction.

\subsection{Evaluation Requirements}
Evaluation must cover several dimensions. Technical validation concerns functional completeness, interoperability, synchronization quality, latency, scalability, reliability, security, privacy, auditability, computational cost, and robustness. Model validation concerns out-of-sample evaluation, external validation, predictive performance, physiological plausibility, calibration, subgroup performance, uncertainty communication, explainability, sensitivity to missing or noisy data, and robustness to dataset shift~\cite{janiesch2021MachineDeepLearning}. AI-enabled twins also require drift monitoring, model audit logs, and revalidation procedures when models or data distributions change~\cite{chaparroCardenas2025DTAIHealthcare,elgammal2025AIPoweredHealthcareDT}.

Clinical validation concerns safety, clinical plausibility, usefulness, prospective evaluation, comparison with accepted clinical practice, and clinician review. Workflow validation concerns usability, dashboard clarity, integration into routine practice, alert fatigue, user trust, human factors, and the effect of the system on care processes~\cite{viceconti2020insilico,amann2020explainability}. In this sense, predictive accuracy is only one part of evaluation; a clinically meaningful Digital Twin must also be understandable, reliable under realistic conditions, integrated into care, and supervised by healthcare professionals.

These findings contribute directly to the general eHealth architecture by defining the layers, data-flow logic, and cross-cutting requirements that any trustworthy eHealth Digital Twin should address. They also prepare the PFE transition by showing why a cardiovascular prototype should focus on multimodal data acquisition, patient-state representation, AI-assisted ECG interpretation, controlled simulation, explainability, and human-in-the-loop decision support.

\section{Research Gap}
The synthesis identifies several unresolved problems. First, there is still no universally accepted definition of healthcare Digital Twins, and maturity classification remains inconsistent. Many systems use the term Digital Twin without demonstrating bidirectional synchronization, continuous state update, or clinically integrated services.

Second, healthcare data remain fragmented, heterogeneous, and difficult to integrate temporally. Interoperability across EHRs, devices, imaging systems, clinical repositories, and patient-generated data remains limited. Missing values, contradictory measurements, biased datasets, weak provenance, and insufficient longitudinal context can reduce the reliability of patient-state representation.

Third, validation remains a major gap. Many works are evaluated retrospectively, conceptually, or on limited proof-of-concept data. External validation, prospective evaluation, clinician review, workflow testing, and accepted healthcare Digital Twin benchmarks are still limited. Patient-specific calibration is difficult, computational cost can be high, and uncertainty quantification is often incomplete. In cardiovascular Digital Twins specifically, additional gaps include small electrophysiology cohorts, imaging-resolution dependence, boundary-condition uncertainty, disease- and drug-specific assumptions, limited multicenter replication, and the difficulty of maintaining an updated patient-specific model across repeated clinical encounters.

Fourth, explainability, privacy, cybersecurity, consent, ownership, fairness, unequal access, responsibility, and regulation remain unresolved. These issues are not secondary concerns because Digital Twins use sensitive longitudinal data and may influence clinical interpretation. The proposed PFE does not claim to solve all these gaps; it explores a subset related to cardiovascular data acquisition, patient-state fusion, ECG diagnostic assistance, controlled what-if simulation, visualization, and human-supervised decision support.

Finally, AI-enabled Digital Twins introduce additional gaps: limited external and prospective evaluation of AI components, insufficient uncertainty quantification, weak monitoring of model drift, limited interpretability of complex models, insufficient multimodal benchmarks, lack of standards for adaptive Digital Twin revalidation, unclear governance of LLM-generated or generative outputs, risk of synthetic-data bias, difficulty distinguishing clinically meaningful personalization from overfitting, and insufficient comparison between pure AI, pure mechanistic, AI-surrogate, and hybrid systems.

\section{Conclusion}
This chapter compared selected Digital Twin studies and extracted conceptual, data-related, modeling, architectural, clinical, cardiovascular, and evaluation findings. The synthesis established the layered structure, cross-cutting requirements, data-flow logic, maturity distinctions, and validation principles required for trustworthy eHealth Digital Twins. The expanded cardiovascular deep-dive showed how electrophysiological, electromechanical, hemodynamic, AI-enhanced, and population-scale cardiac twins illustrate both the scientific potential and the translation limits of the field. The final chapter summarizes the contributions and limitations of the Master study and introduces CardioTwin as a practical cardiovascular specialization of these general findings.
